## Immediately at the Accident Scene

Your first priority is always safety. The steps you take right here, right now, will lay the foundation for everything that follows.

**1. Check for Injuries and Get to Safety**

Before you do anything else, check on yourself and your passengers. Are you hurt? Is anyone else in your vehicle injured? If there are serious injuries, call 911 immediately and do not move anyone who might have a neck or back injury unless they are in immediate danger, like a fire.

If you can, move your vehicle out of traffic to the shoulder or a nearby side street. Turn on your hazard lights to warn other drivers.

**2. Call the Police**

Even if the accident seems minor, always call the police. In California, you are legally required to report an accident to the police or CHP if anyone is injured or killed. California Vehicle Code § 20008 also requires a written report for any accident that causes more than $1,000 in property damage—a number that is easily surpassed in even minor fender-benders today.

A police report is an essential piece of evidence. It provides an official, third-party account of the incident, often including diagrams, witness statements, and the officer’s initial assessment of fault.

**3. Exchange Information (But Say Very Little)**

You must exchange information with the other driver(s) involved. Be calm and polite, but do not apologize or admit any fault. An apology like "I'm so sorry, I didn't see you" can be used against you by an insurance company later, even if the accident wasn't your fault.

Get the following essential information from all drivers involved:

*   Full Name and Address
*   Phone Number
*   Driver's License Number
*   Insurance Company and Policy Number
*   License Plate Number
*   Make, Model, and Color of their Vehicle

If there are witnesses, get their names and phone numbers, too. Their testimony can be invaluable if the other driver changes their story later.

**4. Document Everything with Your Phone**

Your smartphone is your most powerful evidence-gathering tool at the scene. Take more photos and videos than you think you need. Capture:

*   Damage to all vehicles from multiple angles (close-up and wide shots)
*   The position of the cars on the road
*   Skid marks, debris, or other evidence on the pavement
*   Traffic signs, signals, and the general intersection or road layout
*   Any visible injuries you or your passengers have
*   The other driver's license plate, insurance card, and driver's license (if they consent)

These photos create a visual record that can't be disputed later. They help your attorney and accident reconstruction experts piece together exactly what happened.

## In the Hours After the Crash

Once you've left the scene, the clock is still ticking. The next few hours are about prioritizing your health and officially starting the claims process.

**5. Seek Medical Attention—No Matter What**

This is the single most important step you can take. Even if you feel "fine," you need to get checked out by a doctor, at an urgent care clinic, or in an emergency room. Adrenaline from the crash can mask serious pain and injuries for hours or even days.

Common delayed-onset injuries we see in [California car accidents](/car-accidents) include:

*   **Whiplash:** A neck injury from the rapid back-and-forth motion of the head.
*   **Soft Tissue Injuries:** Damage to muscles, ligaments, and tendons.
*   **Concussions and Traumatic Brain Injuries (TBIs):** Even a "minor" bump to the head can have serious consequences.
*   **Spinal Injuries:** Herniated discs or other back problems may not be immediately apparent.

As a former chiropractor who treated thousands of car accident victims, our lead attorney John Reardon knows this better than anyone. He saw firsthand how a seemingly small impact could lead to chronic pain and long-term medical issues. Seeking immediate medical care does two things: it starts your treatment, and it creates a legal link between the accident and your injuries. Without this medical record, an insurance company will argue your injuries weren't caused by the crash.

**6. Report the Accident to Your Own Insurance Company**

Your insurance policy requires you to promptly report any accident. Call the claims number on your insurance card and provide the basic facts: where and when the accident happened, the other driver's information, and the police report number if you have it.

Stick to the facts. Do not guess about who was at fault or downplay your injuries. Simply say you are sore and are seeking medical evaluation. This call officially opens your claim.

## Within the First 24 Hours

You've handled the immediate crisis. Now it's time to protect your legal rights and prepare for the road ahead.

**7. Do NOT Talk to the Other Driver's Insurance Adjuster**

You will likely get a call from the other driver's insurance company very quickly. They may sound friendly and concerned, but their goal is simple: to pay you as little as possible. They will ask to take a recorded statement. Politely decline.

You are under no legal obligation to speak with them. Anything you say in a recorded statement can and will be used to devalue or deny your claim. They are experts at asking leading questions that can trap you into admitting partial fault or minimizing your injuries. Simply tell them, "I will be having my attorney contact you," and hang up.

**8. Start a File and Keep Detailed Notes**

Get a folder or binder and keep everything related to the accident in one place. This includes the police report number, the claim number from your insurance, medical bills, receipts for prescriptions, and any correspondence you receive.

Also, start a simple journal. Each day, write down how you are feeling. What parts of your body hurt? What is your pain level on a scale of 1 to 10? What daily activities are you having trouble with? This detailed record of your pain and suffering will be powerful evidence later.

**9. Understand California's "At-Fault" System**

California is a "pure comparative negligence" state. This is a crucial concept to understand. It means that you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault.

For example, if you are found to be 20% at fault for a crash and your damages are $100,000, you can still recover $80,000. Insurance companies often try to assign a higher percentage of fault to victims to reduce their payout. An experienced attorney can fight to prove the other driver was primarily or entirely responsible.
