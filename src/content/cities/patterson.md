## What to Do After an Accident in Patterson

The actions you take in the minutes and days following an accident are critical for protecting your health and your legal rights. Follow these steps:

1.  **Prioritize Safety and Call 911:** If you can, move your vehicle out of traffic. Check on others involved and call 911 immediately. This will dispatch law enforcement—likely the Patterson Police Department for city incidents or the California Highway Patrol (CHP) for accidents on I-5—and emergency medical services.
2.  **Seek Immediate Medical Evaluation:** Adrenaline can mask serious injuries. Even if you feel fine, get a thorough medical check-up. For severe injuries, you may be transported to a major regional hospital like **Memorial Medical Center** or **Doctors Medical Center of Modesto** to receive appropriate care.
3.  **Document the Scene:** Use your phone to take pictures of all vehicles involved, their damage, license plates, skid marks, road conditions, and your visible injuries. If there are witnesses, ask for their names and contact information.
4.  **Exchange Information:** Get the other driver's name, contact information, driver's license number, insurance company, and policy number. Provide your information but do not discuss fault.
5.  **Be Careful Speaking with Insurance Companies:** You are required to report the accident to your own insurer. However, you should not give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurance adjuster or accept any early settlement offers without first consulting an attorney.
6.  **Contact a Personal Injury Attorney:** A free consultation can provide immediate clarity on your rights and next steps. An experienced lawyer can handle communications with insurance companies and ensure crucial evidence is preserved.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating a personal injury claim requires understanding several key California laws that will directly impact your case.

*   **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover financial compensation even if you were partially at fault for the accident. Your total compensation award will simply be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found 20% at fault, you can still collect 80% of the total damages.
*   **Statute of Limitations:** Time limits for filing a claim are strict. For most personal injury cases, you have **two years** from the date of the injury to file a lawsuit (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). A critical exception exists for claims against government entities (e.g., a city bus, a poorly maintained public road). In these cases, you must file a formal claim within just **six months** under the Government Claims Act.
*   **Minimum Insurance Requirements:** California's mandatory minimum liability insurance is only $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for all injuries in one accident, and $5,000 for property damage. These low amounts are often insufficient to cover the costs of a serious injury, making it vital to identify all potential sources of compensation, including your own uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage.
*   **Available Compensation (Damages):** A successful personal injury claim can provide compensation for both economic and non-economic losses. This can include medical expenses (past and future), lost wages, loss of earning capacity, property damage, pain and suffering, and emotional distress.
