## What to Do After an Accident in Gonzales

The steps you take immediately following an accident are crucial for protecting both your health and your legal rights.

1.  **Prioritize Safety:** If you are able, move your vehicle to a safe location off the main roadway. Check on yourself and your passengers for injuries.
2.  **Call 911:** Report the accident immediately. The California Highway Patrol (CHP) will likely respond to incidents on US-101, while the Gonzales Police Department or Monterey County Sheriff's Office may handle incidents on local streets. An official police report is a vital piece of evidence.
3.  **Seek Medical Attention:** Your health is the top priority. Because there is no hospital in Gonzales, paramedics will likely transport you to a nearby facility like **Natividad Medical Center** in Salinas or **Community Hospital of the Monterey Peninsula**. Even if you feel fine, it is essential to get a medical evaluation, as many serious injuries like whiplash or internal bleeding have delayed symptoms.
4.  **Document the Scene:** Use your phone to take pictures and videos of everything—the position of the cars, damage to all vehicles, skid marks, road conditions, and your injuries.
5.  **Exchange Information:** Get the names, contact information, driver's license numbers, and insurance details from all other drivers involved. If there are witnesses, ask for their names and phone numbers as well.
6.  **Avoid Admitting Fault:** When speaking to the police and other parties, stick to the facts of what happened. Do not apologize, speculate, or accept blame for the accident.
7.  **Contact an Attorney:** Before you give a recorded statement to an insurance adjuster, contact a personal injury lawyer. An experienced attorney can advise you on how to protect your rights and handle all communications with the insurance companies on your behalf.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating the legal system after an injury can be daunting. In California, several key laws will affect your claim.

*   **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can recover compensation even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 20% at fault, your final award will be reduced by 20%.
*   **Statute of Limitations:** There are strict deadlines for filing a lawsuit. For most personal injury cases, California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1 gives you **two years** from the date of the injury to file a claim. However, a critical exception exists for claims against government entities (e.g., a city, county, or state agency). Under the Government Claims Act, you must file a formal notice of claim within just **six months** of the incident. Missing this deadline can permanently bar you from seeking compensation.
*   **California Minimum Insurance:** California law requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of only $15,000 per person injured, $30,000 per accident, and $5,000 for property damage. In any serious accident, these amounts are rarely enough to cover all the damages. An experienced attorney can help you identify all possible sources of recovery, including your own Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage.
*   **Available Compensation:** A successful personal injury claim can provide compensation for economic and non-economic damages, including medical expenses (past and future), lost wages, loss of future earning capacity, property damage, and pain and suffering.
