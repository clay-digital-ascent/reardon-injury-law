## What to Do After an Accident in Bradbury

The moments following an accident are confusing and stressful. Taking the right steps can protect your health and your legal rights. If you are involved in a collision near Bradbury, follow this guide:

1. **Move to Safety and Call 911:** If possible, move your vehicle out of traffic. Call 911 immediately. Depending on your location, the responding agency may be the California Highway Patrol (for accidents on I-210) or the police department of a neighboring city.
2. **Seek Immediate Medical Attention:** Your health is the top priority. Even if you feel you are not seriously hurt, it is crucial to get a medical evaluation. Adrenaline can mask pain from serious injuries. Paramedics may transport you to a nearby facility like **Huntington Hospital** in Pasadena or **Foothill Presbyterian Hospital** in Glendora. A medical record creates a vital link between the accident and your injuries.
3. **Document the Scene:** If you are able, use your phone to take pictures of the vehicles, damage, skid marks, traffic signals, and any visible injuries. Get the names and contact information of any witnesses.
4. **Exchange Information:** Exchange driver's licenses, insurance information, and contact details with the other driver(s). Do not apologize or admit fault.
5. **Report the Accident:** The responding law enforcement officer will create an official report. Be sure to get the report number. This document will be a critical piece of evidence.
6. **Contact an Attorney:** Before you give a recorded statement to any insurance company—especially the other driver's—contact Reardon Injury Law. An adjuster's goal is to minimize their company's payout. We will handle all communications with insurers on your behalf to protect your claim.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating the legal system after an injury can be daunting. As a Bradbury resident, your claim will be governed by California law, and understanding a few key principles is essential.

* **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. Your total compensation will simply be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 20% at fault, you can still collect 80% of the total damages.
* **Statute of Limitations:** In most cases, you have **two years** from the date of the injury to file a personal injury lawsuit (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). However, a critical exception exists for claims against government entities (e.g., being hit by a city bus or tripping on a broken public sidewalk). In these situations, you must file a formal claim within **six months** under the Government Claims Act. Missing these deadlines can permanently bar you from seeking compensation.
* **California's Minimum Insurance Requirements:** The state minimum for liability insurance is only $15,000 per person for bodily injury, $30,000 per accident, and $5,000 for property damage. A serious injury can easily exceed these low limits, which is why it is so important to explore all avenues of recovery, including your own Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist coverage.
* **Available Compensation (Damages):** A successful personal injury claim can provide compensation for both economic and non-economic losses. This includes medical bills (past and future), lost wages, loss of earning capacity, property damage, and non-economic damages for pain, suffering, and emotional distress.
