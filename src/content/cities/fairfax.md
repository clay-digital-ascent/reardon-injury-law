## What to Do After an Accident in Fairfax

The moments following an accident are chaotic and stressful. Taking these steps can protect your health and your legal rights.

1. **Move to Safety and Call 911:** If you are able, move your vehicle out of traffic. Immediately call 911 to report the accident and any injuries. The Fairfax Police Department or the Marin County Sheriff's Office will likely respond to the scene.
2. **Seek Immediate Medical Evaluation:** Your health is the top priority. Accept paramedic assistance or go to an emergency room, even if you feel you are not seriously hurt. Adrenaline can mask pain, and some serious injuries have delayed symptoms. Nearby facilities include **MarinHealth Medical Center** in Greenbrae and **Novato Community Hospital**.
3. **Document the Scene:** If you can do so safely, take pictures and videos of the accident scene, including vehicle damage, skid marks, road conditions, and your injuries. Get the names and contact information of any witnesses.
4. **Exchange Information:** Exchange driver's licenses, insurance policies, and contact information with all other parties involved. Do not apologize or admit fault.
5. **Obtain the Official Report:** The police report is a critical piece of evidence. You can typically request a copy from the responding law enforcement agency a week or two after the incident.
6. **Contact an Attorney:** Before you give a recorded statement to any insurance company, speak with an experienced personal injury lawyer. The insurance adjuster's goal is to minimize their company's payout, not to protect your best interests.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating a personal injury claim requires a clear understanding of California's specific laws. An attorney can help you apply these rules to your case.

* **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" system. This means you can still recover financial compensation even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total award will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 20% at fault, you can still collect 80% of your damages.
* **Statute of Limitations:** Time is limited. In California, you generally have **two years** from the date of the injury to file a personal injury lawsuit (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). Critically, if your claim involves a public or government entity (e.g., a city bus, a dangerous public road), you must file a formal notice of claim within just **six months** under the Government Claims Act.
* **Types of Compensation:** A successful claim can help you recover compensation for both economic and non-economic losses. This includes medical expenses (past and future), lost wages, loss of earning capacity, property damage, pain and suffering, and emotional distress.
* **California's Minimum Insurance:** The state's minimum liability insurance requirements are just $15,000 per person, $30,000 per accident, and $5,000 for property damage. In any serious accident, these low amounts are quickly exhausted, making it vital to explore all avenues for recovery, including your own uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage.
