## What to Do After an Accident in Dana Point

The steps you take immediately following an accident can significantly impact your health and your ability to recover financial compensation.

1.  **Ensure Your Safety:** If possible, move your vehicle out of traffic to a safe location. Turn on your hazard lights to alert other drivers.
2.  **Call 911:** Report the accident immediately. The Orange County Sheriff's Department (OCSD) typically responds to incidents in Dana Point. A police report is a critical piece of evidence for your claim.
3.  **Seek Medical Attention:** Your health is the top priority. Even if you feel fine, it is essential to get a medical evaluation. Adrenaline can mask serious injuries. Paramedics can assess you at the scene, but a follow-up visit at a nearby hospital like **Mission Hospital Mission Viejo** or **Hoag Hospital Newport Beach** is highly recommended. This creates a medical record linking your injuries to the accident.
4.  **Document the Scene:** Use your phone to take pictures and videos of everything: vehicle damage, license plates, skid marks, traffic signals, and your visible injuries.
5.  **Exchange Information:** Calmly obtain the other driver's name, address, phone number, driver's license number, and insurance information. Also, collect contact details from any witnesses.
6.  **Avoid Admitting Fault:** Do not apologize or accept blame for the accident. Stick to the facts when speaking with police and other drivers. Do not give a recorded statement to the other party's insurance adjuster without first consulting an attorney.
7.  **Contact a Dana Point Personal Injury Lawyer:** Before you agree to any settlement, speak with an experienced attorney. We can protect your rights and ensure you don't accept an offer that fails to cover the full cost of your injuries.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating the legal system after an injury can be confusing. It is important to understand the California laws that will govern your claim.

*   **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 20% at fault, your final award will be reduced by 20%.
*   **Statute of Limitations:** In California, you generally have **two years** from the date of the injury to file a personal injury lawsuit (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). Critically, if your claim is against a government entity (like the city, county, or state), you have a much shorter deadline of only **six months** to file a formal claim under the Government Claims Act. Missing these deadlines can permanently bar you from seeking compensation.
*   **Minimum Insurance Requirements:** California law requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of only $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for injury to multiple people, and $5,000 for property damage. In a serious accident, these minimums are often completely inadequate to cover medical bills and lost wages. An experienced attorney can help identify all potential sources of recovery, including your own uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage.
*   **Types of Compensation:** A successful personal injury claim can secure compensation for both economic and non-economic damages. This includes medical expenses (past and future), lost income, property damage, and compensation for your physical pain, mental anguish, and loss of enjoyment of life.
