## What to Do After an Accident in Bakersfield

The steps you take immediately following an accident can significantly impact your health and your ability to recover fair compensation.

1.  **Prioritize Safety and Call 911:** If you can, move your vehicle to a safe location. Check on others involved and call 911 immediately. The Bakersfield Police Department will likely respond to accidents on city streets, while the California Highway Patrol (CHP) handles incidents on highways like CA-99 and I-5.
2.  **Seek Immediate Medical Attention:** Even if you feel fine, it's crucial to get a medical evaluation. Adrenaline can mask serious injuries. Go to an emergency room at a local hospital like **Kern Medical Center** (the area's major trauma facility), **Adventist Health Bakersfield**, or **Mercy Hospital Downtown**. This creates an official record of your injuries.
3.  **Document the Scene:** Use your phone to take pictures and videos of the accident scene, vehicle damage, traffic signals, road conditions, and any visible injuries. If there are witnesses, get their names and contact information.
4.  **Exchange Information:** Exchange driver's license, insurance, and contact information with the other driver(s). Do not apologize or admit fault, as this can be used against you later.
5.  **File an Official Report:** The responding officer will create a traffic collision report. Make sure you get the report number and learn how to obtain a copy.
6.  **Contact an Attorney:** Before you give a recorded statement to an insurance company, speak with a personal injury lawyer. An attorney can protect your rights and handle all communications with the insurance adjusters on your behalf.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating the legal system after an injury can be confusing. It’s important to understand a few key California laws that will affect your claim.

*   **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 10% at fault, your final award will be reduced by 10%.
*   **Statute of Limitations:** In most personal injury cases, you have **two years** from the date of the injury to file a lawsuit (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). If you miss this deadline, you will likely lose your right to sue.
*   **Claims Against Government Entities:** There is a critical exception to the two-year rule. If your injury was caused by a government employee or a dangerous condition on public property (e.g., a poorly maintained road), you must file a formal claim with the correct government agency within **six months** of the incident.
*   **California's Minimum Insurance:** California law only requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for injury to multiple people, and $5,000 for property damage. These low amounts are often insufficient to cover the costs of a serious injury, making it vital to explore all potential sources of recovery, such as your own underinsured motorist coverage.

An experienced attorney can help you secure compensation for medical expenses (past and future), lost wages, loss of earning capacity, and pain and suffering.
