## What to Do After an Accident in La Habra

The moments following an accident are chaotic, but the steps you take can significantly impact your health and your ability to file a successful injury claim.

1.  **Prioritize Safety and Call 911:** If you can, move your vehicle out of traffic. Check on others involved. Call 911 immediately to report the accident and request paramedics. For crashes on city streets like Idaho Street or Whittier Boulevard, the **La Habra Police Department** will likely respond. For incidents on the I-5 or SR-91, the **California Highway Patrol (CHP)** will have jurisdiction.
2.  **Seek Immediate Medical Evaluation:** Even if you feel okay, adrenaline can mask serious injuries. It is crucial to be evaluated by a medical professional. Paramedics can assess you at the scene, but you should also go to an emergency room or your primary doctor. Many La Habra residents are taken to **Providence St. Jude Medical Center** in nearby Fullerton for comprehensive care. This creates an official record of your injuries linked to the accident.
3.  **Document the Scene:** If you are physically able, use your phone to take pictures and videos of the accident scene from multiple angles. Capture vehicle damage, license plates, skid marks, traffic signals, and any visible injuries.
4.  **Exchange Information:** Get the other driver's name, contact information, driver's license number, license plate number, and insurance policy information. Also, collect contact details from any witnesses who saw what happened.
5.  **Do Not Admit Fault:** Avoid saying things like "I'm sorry" or "I think I looked away." These statements can be misinterpreted as an admission of fault by insurance companies. Stick to the facts when speaking with law enforcement.
6.  **Contact a Personal Injury Attorney:** Before you give a recorded statement to any insurance adjuster—even your own—speak with an experienced attorney. The insurance company's goal is to minimize its payout. Your attorney's goal is to protect your rights and ensure you receive fair compensation.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating a personal injury claim requires an understanding of key California laws that will shape your case.

*   **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover compensation even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total compensation award will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are awarded $100,000 but found to be 10% responsible, you would receive $90,000. Insurance companies often try to inflate a victim's share of fault to reduce their payout, which is why having a skilled attorney is vital.
*   **Statute of Limitations:** In California, you have a limited time to file a legal claim. For most personal injury cases, you must file a lawsuit within **two years** of the date of the injury (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). If your claim is against a government entity (e.g., you were hit by a city-owned vehicle or injured by a hazardous road condition), the deadline is much shorter. You must file a formal claim with the public entity within **six months**. Missing these deadlines can permanently bar you from recovering compensation.
*   **Minimum Insurance Requirements:** California's mandatory minimum liability insurance is just $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for injury to multiple people, and $5,000 for property damage. In any serious accident, these amounts are often woefully inadequate to cover medical bills, lost income, and other damages. An experienced attorney can investigate other potential sources of recovery, such as uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage or third-party liability.
*   **Types of Compensation (Damages):** A successful personal injury claim can allow you to recover compensation for both economic and non-economic losses. This can include medical expenses (past and future), lost wages, loss of future earning capacity, property damage, pain and suffering, and emotional distress.
