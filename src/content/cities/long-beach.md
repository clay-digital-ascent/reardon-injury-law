## Accident Hotspots in Long Beach

Long Beach’s unique geography as a major port city, a busy commuter hub, and a popular destination creates several known danger zones for drivers, motorcyclists, and pedestrians. Being aware of these areas is the first step, but if you’ve already been injured in one, you know just how dangerous they can be.

* **The I-710 Freeway Corridor:** As the primary artery connecting the Port of Long Beach to the rest of Southern California, the I-710 is perpetually clogged with semi-trucks, container haulers, and tankers. The sheer size and weight of these vehicles create immense danger. Blind spots, wide turns, falling debris, and overtired drivers contribute to devastating <a href="/truck-accidents">truck accidents</a> along this entire stretch, from the port entrance to its junction with the I-405.

* **The Long Beach Traffic Circle:** A notorious source of confusion and collisions, this roundabout connects Pacific Coast Highway, Lakewood Boulevard, and Los Coyotes Diagonal. The constant merging, lack of clear right-of-way, and high traffic volume lead to frequent sideswipes, T-bone crashes, and rear-end collisions as drivers struggle to navigate the complex flow of traffic.

* **Pacific Coast Highway (PCH) & 7th Street:** PCH is a major thoroughfare through Long Beach, and its intersections are consistently sites of serious accidents. The junction with 7th Street is particularly hazardous due to high speeds and the sheer volume of vehicles. This area poses a significant risk not only for passenger cars but also for <a href="/motorcycle-accidents">motorcycle accidents</a>, as riders are often less visible in the dense traffic.

* **Downtown Streets & Belmont Shore:** Areas like Ocean Boulevard, Pine Avenue, and 2nd Street are dense with restaurants, shops, and attractions. While a vibrant part of the city, this mix of vehicle traffic, cyclists, and people on foot leads to a high number of <a href="/pedestrian-accidents">pedestrian accidents</a> and <a href="/bicycle-accidents">bicycle accidents</a>, especially on evenings and weekends.

## What to Do After an Accident in Long Beach

The moments after an accident are chaotic and stressful. Taking the right steps can protect your health and your legal rights.

1. **Prioritize Safety:** If you can, move your vehicle out of traffic to a safe location. Turn on your hazard lights.
2. **Call 911:** Report the accident immediately. Depending on your location, the Long Beach Police Department (LBPD) will respond to surface streets, while the California Highway Patrol (CHP) handles incidents on freeways like the I-710, I-405, and SR-22.
3. **Seek Medical Attention:** This is the most important step. Request paramedics or go directly to a hospital, even if you feel fine. Adrenaline can mask serious injuries.
