## What to Do After an Accident in Ione

The actions you take in the minutes and days following an accident are critical for protecting your health and your legal rights. Follow these steps:

1.  **Prioritize Safety and Call 911:** Move to a safe location if possible and call 911 immediately. Report your location and any injuries. Depending on where the accident occurred, the Ione Police Department or the California Highway Patrol (CHP) will respond.
2.  **Seek Immediate Medical Evaluation:** Your health is the top priority. Paramedics may transport you to the nearest emergency room, which is likely **Sutter Amador Hospital** in Jackson. For severe, life-threatening injuries, you might be taken to a trauma center like **Mark Twain Medical Center** in San Andreas. Even if you feel fine, it's crucial to get checked out, as some serious injuries have delayed symptoms. You can follow up for non-emergency care at a local clinic like **Amador Valley Foot & Ankle**.
3.  **Document the Scene:** If you are able, use your phone to take pictures and videos of the accident scene, vehicle damage, road conditions, and your injuries. Get the names and contact information of any witnesses.
4.  **Exchange Information:** Exchange driver's licenses, insurance information, and vehicle registration details with the other driver(s). Do not apologize or admit fault.
5.  **File an Official Report:** The responding law enforcement agency will create an accident report. This document is a crucial piece of evidence. You can obtain a copy from the agency that responded to the crash a few days later.
6.  **Contact an Experienced Attorney:** Before you give a recorded statement to any insurance company—even your own—speak with a personal injury lawyer. An attorney can protect you from adjusters whose goal is to minimize your claim and will handle all communications on your behalf.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating a personal injury claim requires an understanding of key California laws. An experienced attorney will manage these legal complexities for you, but it is helpful to be aware of your rights.

*   **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover financial compensation even if you were partially at fault for the accident. Your final award will simply be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 20% responsible, you can still collect 80% of the total damages.
*   **Statute of Limitations:** Time is of the essence. In California, you have a strict deadline, known as the statute of limitations, to file a lawsuit. For most personal injury cases, you have **two years** from the date of the injury (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). However, a critical exception applies to claims against government entities (e.g., a city-owned vehicle, a dangerous public road). Under the Government Claims Act, you must file a formal claim with the public entity within just **six months** of the injury. Missing this deadline can permanently bar you from seeking compensation.
*   **California Minimum Insurance:** The state requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of only $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for injury to multiple people, and $5,000 for property damage. In any serious accident, these amounts are rarely enough to cover medical bills and other losses. Our firm is skilled at identifying all possible sources of recovery, including uninsured/underinsured motorist policies and third-party liability.
*   **Available Compensation:** A successful personal injury claim can provide compensation for both economic and non-economic losses. This includes medical expenses (past and future), lost wages, loss of earning capacity, property damage, pain and suffering, and emotional distress.
