## What to Do After an Accident in Costa Mesa

The actions you take in the minutes and days following an accident are crucial for both your health and your potential legal claim.

1. **Get to Safety and Call 911:** If you are able, move your vehicle out of traffic. Call 911 immediately to report the accident and request police and medical assistance. The Costa Mesa Police Department will likely respond to accidents on surface streets, while the California Highway Patrol (CHP) handles incidents on freeways like the SR-55.
2. **Seek Immediate Medical Attention:** Your health is the top priority. Accept paramedic assistance and go to the emergency room if recommended. Even if you feel fine, it's vital to get a medical evaluation. Adrenaline can mask serious injuries, and a documented medical visit creates a crucial link between the accident and your injuries. Paramedics may transport you to a local hospital such as **Hoag Hospital Newport Beach** or **Orange Coast Memorial Medical Center**.
3. **Document the Scene:** If you are physically able, use your phone to take pictures and videos of the accident scene, including vehicle positions, property damage, skid marks, traffic signals, and your injuries. Get the names and contact information of any witnesses.
4. **Exchange Information:** Exchange driver's license, insurance, and contact information with the other driver(s) involved. Do not argue or apologize. Stick to the facts.
5. **Do Not Give a Recorded Statement:** The other driver’s insurance company may contact you quickly and ask for a recorded statement. Politely decline until you have spoken with an attorney. These statements can be used against you to minimize or deny your claim.
6. **Contact a Personal Injury Attorney:** Before accepting any settlement offer, contact an experienced attorney. We can handle all communications with the insurance companies, manage the legal deadlines, and fight to ensure you receive full and fair compensation.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating a personal injury claim requires an understanding of key California laws that will impact your case.

* **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 10% responsible for a crash, your final award will be reduced by 10%. Insurance companies often use this rule to unfairly shift blame and reduce payouts, which is why having an attorney to fight back is essential.
* **Statute of Limitations:** In California, you have a limited time to file a legal claim. For most personal injury cases, the statute of limitations is **two years** from the date of the injury (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). However, there is a critical exception: if your claim is against a government entity (e.g., a city bus, a police vehicle, or an injury caused by a poorly maintained public road), you must file a formal claim within **six months** under the Government Claims Act. Missing these deadlines can permanently bar you from recovering compensation.
* **California's Minimum Insurance Requirements:** The state requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of only $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for injury to multiple people, and $5,000 for property damage. In any serious accident, these minimums are rarely enough to cover the full cost of medical bills and lost wages. An experienced attorney can help you explore other potential sources of recovery, such as your own Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage or assets from the at-fault party.
* **Recoverable Damages:** Victims of negligence are entitled to seek compensation for economic and non-economic losses, including past and future medical expenses, lost wages and earning capacity, property damage, and pain and suffering.
