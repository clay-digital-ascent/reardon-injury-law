## What to Do After an Accident in Corte Madera

The steps you take immediately following an accident are crucial for protecting both your health and your legal rights.

1.  **Ensure Safety and Call 911:** If you are able, move to a safe location away from traffic. Call 911 immediately to report the accident and request emergency medical services. Depending on your location, the Marin County Sheriff's Office or the California Highway Patrol (CHP) will likely respond.
2.  **Seek Immediate Medical Attention:** Your health is the top priority. Accept paramedic assistance and go to the emergency room if recommended. Even if you feel fine, it is vital to get a medical evaluation. Adrenaline can mask serious injuries. Go to a nearby hospital like **Kaiser Permanente San Rafael Medical Center** or **MarinHealth Medical Center**. For catastrophic injuries, you may be transported to a Level 1 trauma center like **UCSF Health Medical Center at St. Mary's Campus** in San Francisco.
3.  **Document the Scene:** If you are physically able, take photos and videos of the accident scene from multiple angles. Capture vehicle damage, license plates, skid marks, road conditions, and any relevant traffic signs.
4.  **Exchange Information:** Get the names, contact information, driver's license numbers, and insurance details of all drivers involved. Also, collect contact information from any witnesses.
5.  **Do Not Admit Fault:** Avoid saying anything that could be interpreted as an admission of guilt, such as "I'm sorry" or "I didn't see you." Stick to the facts when speaking with law enforcement.
6.  **Contact a Personal Injury Attorney:** Before you speak with any insurance adjusters, contact an experienced attorney. The insurance company's goal is to minimize its payout. Your attorney's goal is to protect your interests and ensure you are compensated fairly.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating a personal injury claim requires a clear understanding of California's specific laws. An attorney can guide you through these complexities, but here are some key principles that may affect your case.

*   **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your total compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 10% responsible for a crash, you can recover 90% of your total damages.
*   **Statute of Limitations:** In California, you generally have **two years** from the date of the injury to file a personal injury lawsuit (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). If you miss this deadline, you will likely lose your right to seek compensation forever. There is a critical exception: if your claim is against a government entity (e.g., a city bus, a poorly maintained public road), you must file a formal claim within just **six months** under the Government Claims Act.
*   **California Minimum Insurance:** The state's minimum liability insurance requirements are very low: $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for injury to multiple people, and $5,000 for property damage. In any serious accident, these amounts are rarely enough to cover the full cost of medical bills and lost wages, making it vital to identify all possible sources of recovery, including your own Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist coverage.
*   **Recoverable Damages:** Victims of negligence are entitled to seek compensation for both economic and non-economic losses. This can include medical expenses (past and future), lost income, property damage, as well as pain and suffering, emotional distress, and loss of enjoyment of life.
