## What to Do After an Accident in Corona

The steps you take immediately following an accident are crucial for protecting your health and your legal rights.

1. **Prioritize Safety:** If possible, move your vehicle out of traffic to a safe location. Turn on your hazard lights to alert other drivers.
2. **Call 911:** Report the accident to the authorities. For incidents on surface streets within Corona, the Corona Police Department will likely respond. For crashes on the SR-91, I-15, or other freeways, the California Highway Patrol (CHP) has jurisdiction.
3. **Seek Medical Attention:** Your health is the top priority. Accept emergency medical services at the scene or go to the nearest hospital, such as Corona Regional Medical Center or a regional trauma center like Riverside Community Hospital. Some serious injuries have delayed symptoms, so it’s vital to get a medical evaluation even if you feel fine.
4. **Document the Scene:** If you are able, use your phone to take pictures of the vehicles, the accident scene, any visible injuries, and relevant road signs or conditions.
5. **Exchange Information:** Get the other driver’s name, contact information, driver’s license number, license plate number, and insurance details. Also, collect contact information from any witnesses.
6. **Do Not Admit Fault:** Avoid saying "I'm sorry" or accepting blame. Stick to the facts when speaking with police. Do not give a recorded statement to the other party’s insurance adjuster without first consulting an attorney.
7. **Contact an Attorney:** Before you navigate the complex claims process, contact an experienced Corona personal injury lawyer. We can help you preserve evidence, handle communications with insurance companies, and ensure you take the right steps to build a strong case.

## Understanding Your Rights Under California Law

Navigating a personal injury claim requires an understanding of California's specific laws. An experienced attorney can guide you, but here are some key principles that may apply to your case:

* **Pure Comparative Negligence:** California follows a "pure comparative negligence" rule. This means you can still recover damages even if you were partially at fault for the accident. However, your final compensation will be reduced by your percentage of fault. For example, if you are found to be 20% responsible, your total award will be reduced by 20%.
* **Statute of Limitations:** There are strict deadlines for filing a personal injury lawsuit. In most cases, you have **two years** from the date of the injury to file a claim (California Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1). A critical exception exists for claims against government entities (e.g., a city bus, a poorly maintained public road). In these situations, you must file a formal claim with the government agency within just **six months** of the injury.
* **California's Minimum Insurance:** The state's minimum liability insurance requirements are just $15,000 for injury to one person, $30,000 for injury to multiple people, and $5,000 for property damage. These low limits are often insufficient to cover the costs of a serious injury, which makes it crucial to explore all avenues for compensation, including your own Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist coverage.
* **Types of Compensation:** As a victim, you may be entitled to recover damages for a wide range of losses, including past and future medical expenses, lost wages, diminished earning capacity, property damage, and non-economic damages like pain, suffering, and emotional distress.
